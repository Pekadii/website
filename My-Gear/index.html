ND64 Filter

A 6-stop neutral density filter for controlling light without changing color.

Bright daylight Video shutter control Long exposure Wide aperture
  • Purpose: reduces light entering the lens so you can use slower shutter speeds or wider apertures in bright conditions.
  • For video: helps keep natural motion blur, such as 1/50 shutter for 24fps or 1/60 for 30fps, even outside.
  • For photos: useful for smoothing water, adding motion blur, or shooting shallow depth of field in harsh sunlight.
  • ND64 strength: cuts about 6 stops of light, which is strong enough for bright outdoor scenes.

ND Filter Types

Fixed ND

One strength, such as ND8, ND64, or ND1000. Best image quality and predictable color.

Variable ND

Adjustable strength in one filter. Convenient for video, but cheaper ones can add color shifts or cross patterns.

Graduated ND

Darker on one side, clear on the other. Helps balance bright skies with darker foregrounds.

Money-saving tip: buy one larger filter size and use step-up rings for smaller lenses. It is usually cheaper than buying the same ND filter in every lens thread size.

Kit Lens vs “Fast” Lens (Why Aperture Matters)

A typical kit lens usually has a smaller maximum aperture (for example f/3.5–5.6), which means it lets in less light. In darker scenes, you’ll often need a higher ISO (more grain) or a slower shutter (more motion blur).

A fast lens (like f/1.8 or f/2.8) lets in much more light, so you can shoot cleaner at night, keep your shutter faster, and get more background blur for portraits.

  • Kit lens: great starter, travel, daylight — sharp enough, but needs more light.
  • Fast lens: better low-light + more subject separation (blurred background).

Tip: a lens with a “higher f-number” (like f/5.6) isn’t “better” — it just means less light enters the lens.

Real-world example: Basic lenses aren’t bad — they just require understanding light and timing.
